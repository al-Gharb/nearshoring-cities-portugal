Executive Summary

Portugal has emerged as a compelling nearshoring destination for European technology firms. With ~30,000 annual Digital STEM+ graduates https://www.portugalglobal.pt/en/investment/why-portugal/reasons-to-invest/talent/, a mature startup ecosystem (€7.5B+ in Porto alone), and fully-loaded engineer costs 30–40% below major Western European hubs, the country offers a rare combination: EU legal framework, strong English proficiency (ranked 7th globally), same-timezone collaboration, and infrastructure-grade digital connectivity (98% 5G, new subsea cables: EllaLink live, Medusa underway).

Labour Market Considerations

Despite strong graduate output, the EU Education Monitor 2025 notes persistent labour shortages in Engineering & ICT roles. Key factors to consider:

  • Competitive hiring: Lisbon & Porto have active competition from multinationals, scale-ups, and consultancies
  • Salary trends: Senior developer compensation has grown steadily since 2021
  • Retention planning: Average tech tenure is 18-24 months in major hubs — plan accordingly
  • Remote competition: Some international companies hire Portuguese talent remotely at higher rates
  • Regional variation: Lisbon 53% higher education attainment vs. Azores 22% — talent distribution varies

Digital STEM+ Methodology and the particularities of the Portuguese educational system

This document uses a Digital STEM+ definition (~27-30k graduates/year) rather than the narrower Eurostat Core STEM figure (~19k). Both are valid—the broader definition reflects the practical hiring-relevant talent pool for technology employers.

Why Digital STEM+ Instead of Core STEM?

Core STEM (~19k) captures traditional science and engineering graduates. But tech employers also hire from adjacent programs that don't fit Eurostat's strict classification—these graduates have the skills companies need even if their degrees aren't labeled "engineering" or "computer science."

Adjacent Digital (~5k) represents the largest expansion factor. This includes: Information Systems (business-tech hybrid programs), UX/UI Design (digital product roles), Technology Management (tech-focused business degrees), Digital Health/Bioinformatics (healthcare IT crossover), and Data Analytics programs housed in business schools rather than engineering faculties. These graduates fill product, operations, and technical roles at tech companies.

CTeSP: Short-Cycle Technical Diplomas (~2-3k/year)

Cursos Técnicos Superiores Profissionais—2-year programs (120 ECTS) at polytechnics. Practical focus: Web Development, Programming, IT Support, Cybersecurity. Graduates receive a "Higher Technician Diploma" (EQF Level 5) and often continue to bachelor's degrees. A modest but fast-track pipeline to entry-level tech roles.

Counting Differences: Portugal vs. Germany
Portugal
  • CTeSP (2-year) counts as higher education (EQF 5)
  • Adjacent programs (Info Systems, UX) often in university system
  • Core STEM → Digital STEM+ expansion: ~1.4-1.6×
Germany
  • Ausbildung/Dual system tracked separately from HE statistics
  • Adjacent roles often filled by Fachhochschule or vocational paths
  • Core STEM → broader expansion: ~1.2-1.3×
Germany's "Hidden" Technical Workforce: The German Ausbildung system produces thousands of ICT specialists (Fachinformatiker, etc.) not counted in university graduate stats. When comparing "graduates," Portugal's 9 cities (~26k) appear comparable to Munich (~15k). But Germany's total market-ready talent is ~30-40% higher than graduate stats suggest—this vocational layer is invisible in ISCED/Eurostat comparisons.

Bottom line: This is a classification difference, not a quality difference. Germany's vocational system produces highly skilled technicians—they're just counted differently. For business decisions, consider both systems' outputs; for academic comparisons, stick to Core STEM definitions.

How This Matrix Works — Methodology & Value

Calculation Methodology:

  • X-Axis (Cost Pressure Index): Combines the Numbeo Cost of Living + Rent Index (consumer-weighted, includes residential rent) with average office rent (€/m²/month) as a proxy for relative cost pressure. Note: This is not a comprehensive business cost calculation—it excludes wages, payroll taxes, and productivity factors. Range: 38.5 (Covilhã) to 70.5 (Lisbon). Midpoint at 50.
  • Y-Axis (Annual Talent Flow): Uses a logarithmic scale based on annual Digital STEM+ graduates (850–9,500). Important: This measures new graduate output, not total available workforce. Cities with strong retention or in-migration may have larger effective talent pools than graduate counts suggest. Formula: Y = 68 − ((ln(grads) − ln(850)) / (ln(9500) − ln(850))) × 60.
  • Outer Bubble Size (Digital STEM+): Linear scale proportional to total broad Digital STEM+ graduates (Sciences + ICT + Engineering, CNAEF 05+06+07): size = 42 + (grads/9500) × 88px, making overall talent pool size immediately visible.
  • Inner Core Size (Core ICT): Proportional to pure Core ICT graduates (CS + Informatics + Networks)—a subset of Digital STEM+, not additive: core = 20 + (ict/2900) × 40px, highlighting the digital tech-specific concentration within each city.
  • Benchmark Labels: Each bubble shows comparison vs Lisbon (the largest hub): "−X% cost | −Y% talent" indicates how much cheaper and smaller the talent pool is relative to Lisbon.

Quadrant Classification:

  • Low Cost / High Talent (Green): Lower cost pressure with higher graduate output
  • High Cost / High Talent (Yellow): Higher cost pressure with largest graduate pools
  • Low Cost / Low Talent (Orange): Lowest cost pressure with smaller graduate output
  • High Cost / Low Talent (Blue): Higher cost pressure with smaller graduate pools

Why This Matters: Traditional site selection relies on single metrics (salary or rent). This matrix provides a multi-dimensional view capturing the true cost-talent trade-off. The double-bubble design shows both total Digital STEM+ capacity (outer ring) and Core ICT depth (inner core), helping decision-makers understand both breadth and tech-specific focus. The logarithmic Y-axis prevents large cities from dominating the visual, giving mid-tier university towns fair representation.

Workforce Talent Pools

This section analyzes the existing experienced workforce—professionals already working in tech who may be available for hiring. Unlike graduate flow (new talent entering the market), workforce pools represent immediate hiring potential with proven skills and industry experience.

Portugal in the EU Context

According to Eurostat (April 2025), Portugal ranks among the EU's top performers for ICT specialist employment, with 5.1% of total employment in ICT roles—above the EU average of 5.0%. The country has seen one of the fastest growth rates in female ICT specialists (+11.0% annually, 2014–2024) and a significant increase in tertiary-educated ICT workers (+18 pp over the decade). Notably, 44.3% of Portugal's ICT specialists are aged 15–34, indicating a younger workforce compared to most EU peers.

Note: "ICT" in this section refers to Eurostat's occupation-based definition (employed ICT specialists). This differs from our "Core ICT" graduate metric, which uses the education-based CNAEF classification (code 06 only). Both measure digital/tech talent, but from different angles: workforce vs. graduate pipeline.
